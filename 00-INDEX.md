## Start Here: Top 3 Ideas to Build First

These three recommendations balance speed-to-market, recession resilience, revenue clarity, competitive gaps, and viral potential. They are ordered by how quickly a solo developer can ship an MVP and start generating revenue.

### 1. ADHD Task Decomposer (Rank #3 — Score 88)

The ADHD Task Decomposer is the single best idea to build first because it combines the fastest MVP timeline (5 weeks) with the most passionate, vocal user community on the internet. The 2.1 million members of r/ADHD, combined with ADHD TikTok (a massive content vertical where creators regularly go viral sharing tools and coping strategies), create a distribution channel that money cannot buy. Goblin Tools proved explosive demand by going viral with a simple AI task decomposition feature — but it stops there. It has no workflow, no persistence, no timers, no energy matching, no body-doubling, and no streak mechanics. That gap is the opportunity.

The recession resistance is ironclad: ADHD does not follow economic cycles, and productivity tools become more critical when job security is threatened. The $6.99/mo price point sits in the sweet spot — accessible enough for the target audience, high enough to build a real business. At 30,000 paid users by Year 3 ($2.5M ARR), this is a venture-scale outcome achievable through organic virality alone. The "this app changed my life" sharing mechanic is built into the product's DNA: when someone with ADHD finally completes a task they have been paralyzed by for weeks, they tell everyone. Energy-level matching — reordering micro-steps based on how the user feels right now — is a clinically-informed innovation that no competitor offers and that creates genuine product differentiation.

The MVP is straightforward: AI decomposition + energy rating + a simple timer + "just start" mode showing only the next step. No integrations, no social features, no elaborate gamification — just the core loop of "enter overwhelming task, get micro-steps, start doing them." Ship it, seed it in r/ADHD and ADHD TikTok, and iterate based on the community's extremely specific and vocal feedback.

### 2. Failed Payment Recovery (Rank #2 — Score 90)

Failed Payment Recovery (DunningGuard) is the highest-revenue opportunity in the entire playbook, projecting $2.4M ARR by Year 3, and it benefits from the clearest willingness to pay of any idea on this list. B2B SaaS companies losing 9% of MRR to failed payments do not need to be convinced that recovering that revenue is worth paying for — the ROI conversation is trivially easy. Performance-based pricing (you only pay when money is recovered) eliminates purchase objections entirely and aligns your incentives with your customers' outcomes.

The competitive landscape has a rare structural gap. Baremetrics Recover shut down. ProfitWell Retain was acquired by Paddle and is now restricted to Paddle-only customers. Churnkey focuses on voluntary churn (cancellation flows), not involuntary churn (failed payments). Stunning.co offers basic dunning emails without smart retry optimization. The SMB tier — SaaS companies with $50K-$500K MRR — is underserved by every remaining player. Multi-processor support (Stripe + Paddle + Braintree) is a concrete differentiator when most tools only integrate with Stripe. The 8-week MVP timeline is longer than the ADHD app but still very manageable for a solo developer, and the switching costs created by multi-processor integration plus accumulated performance data make this a genuinely sticky product (4/5 moat strength).

The go-to-market is straightforward B2B: find SaaS founders losing money to failed payments (they are everywhere on Twitter/X, Indie Hackers, and SaaS-focused communities), show them exactly how much MRR they are losing, and offer to recover it with no upfront cost. Every recovered dollar is a testimonial waiting to happen.

### 3. Landlord Ledger (Rank #4 — Score 87)

Landlord Ledger targets 10.6 million individual landlords in the US, 72% of whom own fewer than 5 units and overwhelmingly use spreadsheets or shoeboxes for accounting. The enterprise tools (AppFolio at $280/mo, Buildium at $55/mo plus per-unit fees) are absurdly overpriced for someone managing a duplex. Stessa, the closest free alternative, was acquired by Roofstock and is pivoting toward investor tools, leaving a widening gap. The killer feature — one-click Schedule E tax report generation with expenses auto-categorized by IRS line items — saves landlords $200-500 in CPA fees annually, making the $9.99/mo subscription an obvious value proposition.

The moat here is exceptionally strong (4/5) for a product at this price point. Once a landlord enters their properties, tenants, lease terms, mortgage details, and a year of categorized expenses, switching to another tool means re-entering all of that data. This creates the kind of quiet, durable retention that subscription businesses dream about — landlords simply do not switch accounting tools. The 7-week MVP timeline is moderate, and the community distribution channels are well-defined: r/landlord (350K members), BiggerPockets forums, and local real estate investor meetups are filled with the exact target user asking "what do you use to track rent and expenses?"

At $1.5M ARR by Year 3 with 12,500 subscribers, Landlord Ledger is a realistic, grounded projection. The path from MVP to that number runs through a referral program (landlords know other landlords), SEO targeting "landlord accounting software" and "Schedule E tax software," and partnerships with property management courses and real estate investing communities. The recession resistance is airtight: rental properties do not disappear in downturns, and proper tax documentation becomes more important when margins tighten.
