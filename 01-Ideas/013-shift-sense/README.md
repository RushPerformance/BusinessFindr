# 13. ShiftSense

> Employee scheduling for small teams that accounts for labor law compliance automatically

| Field | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Category** | SMB Tools / B2B SaaS |
| **Target Audience** | Restaurants, retail stores, clinics, salons with 5-50 hourly employees |
| **Monetization** | Per-employee pricing ($3-5/employee/mo) |
| **Recession Resistance** | High - shift-based businesses operate in all economies; labor law penalties make compliance non-optional |
| **Build Effort** | Medium - 10-12 weeks MVP |

## Problem

33% of small businesses still use paper or spreadsheets for scheduling (SCORE 2024). Predictive scheduling laws now exist in 10+ US cities/states (NYC, LA, Chicago, Oregon, etc.) with fines of $75-500 per violation per employee. A restaurant with 20 employees can rack up $50K+ in fines annually for schedule change violations alone.

**Reddit signal**: r/smallbusiness and r/restaurateur threads repeatedly cite scheduling as a top-3 operational headache. Common complaints: "When I Work is too expensive," "Homebase keeps raising prices," "none of them handle [city] fair workweek law."

## Solution

Drag-and-drop scheduling with built-in labor law guardrails. Auto-flags predictive scheduling violations before posting. Shift swap marketplace for employees. Overtime alerts. Time clock with GPS.

## Market Size

$2.8B workforce management software market for SMBs (MarketsandMarkets). Employee scheduling subset: ~$900M.

## Revenue Potential

2,000 locations x 20 employees x $4/employee/mo = $1.9M ARR. Expand with time tracking and payroll integrations.

## Existing Competitors

When I Work, Homebase, 7shifts, Deputy. Common complaints: price creep, poor predictive scheduling law support, clunky UX for non-tech managers.

## Competitive Moat

Jurisdiction-specific labor law engine that stays current. This is the hardest part to build and the biggest reason to switch. Compliance = lock-in.
