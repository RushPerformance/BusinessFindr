## The Problem
Food waste makes up 22% of US landfill content — 63 million tons per year (EPA). An estimated 72% of Americans don't compost, and the #1 reason is "I don't have space or the right setup" (National Waste & Recycling Association). The $1.8B composting market is rapidly expanding as cities pass food waste diversion mandates — California (SB 1383), Vermont, New York City, Seattle, San Francisco, and others now require or incentivize composting. But finding where to compost is surprisingly difficult: community drop-off sites, curbside pickup services, farmers market collection points, and composting classes are scattered across municipal websites, nonprofit pages, and social media with no centralized directory. A person who wants to start composting in Brooklyn faces a different set of options than someone in Portland, and discovering those options takes hours of research.

## The Solution
1. Composting site map: searchable directory of community composting drop-off locations with hours, accepted materials, and user reviews
2. Pickup service finder: local compost collection services with pricing, frequency, and sign-up links
3. Accepted materials guide: each location's specific rules (some accept meat/dairy, others don't)
4. Composting class finder: workshops, webinars, and Master Composter programs in your area
5. City mandate tracker: know your local composting requirements and compliance deadlines
6. Newcomer onboarding: "I want to start composting" flow that matches you with the best local option for your situation
7. Kitchen scrap tracking: log what you're diverting from landfill with impact metrics (CO2 prevented, landfill weight saved)
8. Community features: connect with nearby composters, share tips, and organize community collection drives

## Existing Alternatives
- **ShareWaste**: Connects food scrap producers with composters, but limited to peer-to-peer and sparse coverage in many cities
- **Municipal websites**: List some composting sites but are hard to navigate, often outdated, and don't cover private services
- **Google Maps search for "composting near me"**: Returns random results with no information about accepted materials or hours
- **Compost Now / Bootstrap Compost**: Regional pickup services with their own apps, but each covers only one metro area
- **Zero-waste blogs / Instagram**: Inspiration but not practical tools for finding local composting access

## Why This Wins
- Centralized directory solves the #1 barrier to composting for apartment dwellers and people without yards
- Regulatory tailwinds: as more cities mandate composting, demand for this tool grows automatically
- Accepted materials guide prevents the frustration of showing up with the wrong scraps
- Kitchen scrap tracking provides motivating feedback ("You've diverted 50 lbs from the landfill this month")
- Low-cost to build and maintain — primarily a directory/database with location services
