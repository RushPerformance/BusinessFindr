## Problem

An estimated 2.5 million people die in the US each year, leaving behind an average of 5 close grievers per death — approximately 12.5 million Americans actively grieving at any time. The American Psychological Association reports that 7-10% of bereaved adults develop prolonged grief disorder (PGD), now a recognized clinical diagnosis in the DSM-5-TR. Even among those without clinical PGD, 68% report that grief significantly impacts their daily functioning for 6+ months. Yet only 25% of grieving individuals seek professional support, primarily due to cost ($150-$250/session for grief counseling) and stigma. Research published in the Journal of Clinical Psychology found that structured grief journaling reduces symptoms of complicated grief by 32% and improves sleep quality by 41%. The challenge is that general journaling apps offer no grief-specific structure, and the few grief apps that exist focus on meditation rather than the evidence-based practice of written processing.

---

## Solution

1. **Therapeutic Grief Prompts** — Daily journaling prompts grounded in evidence-based grief therapy frameworks (Dual Process Model, Continuing Bonds theory) that gently guide processing without prescribing stages.
2. **Milestone Calendar** — Tracks and prepares users for difficult dates: birthdays, anniversaries, holidays, death anniversary — with targeted prompts and coping strategies before each date arrives.
3. **Memory Preservation** — Dedicated space to record memories, upload photos, and capture stories about the person who died, creating a living memorial within the journal.
4. **Mood and Symptom Tracking** — Daily check-ins tracking grief intensity, sleep quality, appetite, social engagement, and energy levels with trend visualization over weeks and months.
5. **Therapist Sharing Portal** — Optional feature to share selected journal entries and mood data with a therapist or counselor, enriching clinical sessions with between-session data.
6. **Grief Community Connection** — Moderated, anonymous peer support forums organized by type of loss (spouse, parent, child, friend, pet) for those who want connection without judgment.
7. **Crisis Resources** — Built-in crisis detection that surfaces helpline numbers and resources when journal entries or mood data indicate acute distress.
8. **Progress Reflections** — Monthly and quarterly reflections that help users see their own resilience by comparing current entries to earlier ones without minimizing ongoing grief.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Calm/Headspace** — Offer grief meditation content but no structured journaling, no milestone tracking, and no therapist integration.
2. **Grief.com (David Kessler)** — Provides grief resources and online courses but no daily journaling tool or personalized tracking.
3. **Apart of Me** — A grief app designed as a game for young people; not suitable for adult grief processing or clinical integration.
4. **Day One / Journey (General Journals)** — Powerful journaling apps but with no grief-specific prompts, milestone tracking, or clinical frameworks.
5. **Grief Support Groups (GriefShare)** — In-person and virtual groups are valuable but meet weekly and do not support daily processing between sessions.

## Why This Wins

- **Evidence-based approach** — structured grief journaling has clinical evidence for effectiveness, unlike general meditation or unguided journaling.
- **Milestone anticipation is unique** — no other app proactively prepares grievers for difficult upcoming dates with targeted support.
- **Therapist integration creates clinical distribution** — grief counselors can recommend the app to all their clients, creating a low-cost professional referral channel.
- **Recession-proof demand** — death and grief are constants regardless of economic conditions; demand is entirely non-cyclical.
- **Long engagement cycle** — grief journaling can continue for years; active grievers have some of the highest retention rates in wellness apps.
