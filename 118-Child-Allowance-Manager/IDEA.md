# Child Allowance Manager

**One-Line Pitch:** A digital allowance system where kids earn money through chores, set savings goals, learn spending limits, and get age-appropriate financial literacy lessons — building money skills before they get a real bank account.

## Problem

A T. Rowe Price survey found that 69% of parents feel awkward talking about money with their kids, and only 23% of children in the US receive any financial education before high school. The Council for Economic Education reports that just 23 states require a personal finance course for high school graduation, leaving millions of children financially illiterate entering adulthood. Meanwhile, cash is disappearing — 80% of transactions are now digital, making the traditional "cash in a jar" allowance system obsolete. Kids who never handle physical money miss the tangible experience of spending, saving, and watching balances grow or shrink. Studies from the University of Cambridge show that money habits are largely formed by age 7, yet most financial tools (Greenlight, GoHenry) require a full banking relationship with debit cards, fees, and minimum balances — overkill for a 6-year-old earning $5/week for making their bed. Parents need a simpler, lighter tool focused on teaching concepts before introducing real banking products.

---

## Solution

1. **Virtual Wallet** — Each child gets a virtual balance visible to both parent and child, with separate "spending," "saving," and "giving" buckets that parents configure.
2. **Chore-Based Earning** — Parents create chore lists with dollar amounts; kids check off completed chores for parent approval, and earnings automatically add to their balance.
3. **Savings Goals** — Kids set visual savings goals (new toy, video game, bike) with progress bars and milestone celebrations when they hit 25%, 50%, 75%, and 100%.
4. **Spending Requests** — Kids request to spend from their balance; parents approve or deny with an optional teaching moment ("Are you sure you want to spend half your savings on this?").
5. **Interest Simulator** — Parents can set a virtual "interest rate" on savings (e.g., 10% monthly match) to teach the concept of compound interest in an accelerated, tangible way.
6. **Financial Literacy Lessons** — Age-appropriate bite-sized lessons on earning, saving, spending, sharing, needs vs. wants, and delayed gratification.
7. **Family Leaderboard** — Siblings can see (optionally) each other's savings progress, creating healthy competition and motivation.
8. **Weekly Summary** — Parents receive a weekly email summarizing each child's earnings, spending, and savings progress.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Greenlight** — Full debit card and banking platform for kids but costs $4.99-$9.99/mo, requires SSN, and is complex for families with young children (under 8).
2. **GoHenry** — Similar to Greenlight with debit cards and financial education but $3.99/mo per child (expensive for multi-child families) and UK-focused.
3. **FamZoo** — Virtual family bank with prepaid cards but clunky interface, $5.99/mo, and has not been significantly updated in years.
4. **Cash/Physical Allowance** — Still used by many families but becoming impractical as cash transactions decline and does not teach digital money management.
5. **Chore Chart Apps (OurHome, ChoreMonster)** — Track chores but have no financial component, savings goals, or financial literacy content.

## Why This Wins

- **Simpler and cheaper than card-based alternatives** — no debit card, no SSN required, no per-child pricing; works for a 5-year-old learning basic concepts.
- **Education-first positioning** — focuses on building money habits and financial literacy rather than spending enablement, resonating with intentional parents.
- **Multi-child flat pricing** — $3.99/mo for the whole family regardless of number of children, unlike competitors charging per child.
- **Interest simulator is unique** — no competitor lets parents set virtual interest rates to teach compound interest in an accelerated, tangible way.
- **School and homeschool distribution** — financial literacy curriculum partnerships create institutional adoption channels beyond direct-to-parent marketing.
