## Problem

The average American household has $50,000-$100,000 worth of personal property, yet the Insurance Information Institute reports that only 55% of homeowners have a home inventory and only 27% have one that is up to date. This matters most after a disaster — FEMA data shows that 40% of businesses and households affected by natural disasters never recover their full insured value due to inability to prove what they owned. Beyond insurance, the average American garage contains 300+ items, and 25% of Americans with two-car garages cannot park even one car inside due to accumulated belongings (US Department of Energy). The self-storage industry has grown to $39 billion because people have so much stuff they cannot even catalog what they have. Storage unit owners frequently forget what they are storing, paying an average of $180/month for items they cannot remember. A simple inventory tool with photos and values could save thousands in insurance claims and help people decide what to keep, sell, or donate.

---

## Solution

1. **Photo-Based Inventory** — Snap photos of items and the app uses image recognition to suggest categories, names, and estimated values.
2. **Category Organization** — Pre-built categories (tools, sports equipment, holiday decorations, automotive, electronics) with custom categories for unique collections.
3. **Insurance Valuation** — Estimated replacement value for each item based on category and condition, with a running total for insurance documentation.
4. **Location Tracking** — Tag items by location (garage shelf A, storage unit 147, attic, basement) so you can find things without digging through everything.
5. **Barcode/Serial Number Scanner** — Scan barcodes and serial numbers for electronics, tools, and equipment for warranty claims and theft recovery.
6. **Insurance Report Generator** — One-click generation of a formatted inventory report with photos and values suitable for insurance claims or policy reviews.
7. **Lending Tracker** — Record when you lend items to neighbors or friends with reminders to get them back.
8. **Declutter Mode** — Flags items not accessed in 12+ months and suggests selling (with estimated resale value), donating, or discarding.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Sortly** — Home inventory app but designed for small businesses managing physical inventory; $29/mo pricing is too expensive for personal use.
2. **Encircle** — Insurance-focused home inventory but sold to insurance adjusters (B2B), not to consumers for proactive inventorying.
3. **Home Contents (Allstate Digital Locker)** — Basic inventory tool but limited to Allstate customers and lacks photo organization and valuation features.
4. **Spreadsheets/Notes** — The most common approach; no photos, no valuation estimates, and abandoned after initial enthusiasm.
5. **Video Walk-Through** — Some insurers recommend filming each room; better than nothing but impossible to search, update, or generate reports from.

## Why This Wins

- **Insurance claim urgency** — one disaster proves the app's value; users who have experienced a claim are the most enthusiastic advocates.
- **Photo-first UX** — making inventory as easy as taking pictures removes the friction that kills spreadsheet-based approaches.
- **Declutter trend alignment** — the KonMari and minimalism movements have millions of people wanting to catalog and reduce their possessions.
- **Storage unit ROI** — showing users they are paying $180/mo to store $200 worth of forgotten items is a powerful value proposition.
- **Insurance company partnerships** — insurers want policyholders to have inventories (reduces fraudulent claims and speeds legitimate ones), creating a B2B distribution channel.
