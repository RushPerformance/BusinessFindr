## Problem

There are 46.2 million foreign-born people living in the US, including 13.6 million lawful permanent residents (green card holders), 3.5 million on temporary visas, and 1.7 million refugees and asylees. The National Immigration Law Center estimates that eligible immigrants leave $28 billion in federal benefits unclaimed annually because they either do not know they qualify or fear that using benefits will jeopardize their immigration status. The complexity is staggering: benefit eligibility varies by immigration status (green card, work visa, refugee, asylee, TPS, DACA), duration of residency (5-year bar for many programs), state of residence (states have different SNAP, Medicaid, and CHIP policies for immigrants), and family composition (US-citizen children of immigrant parents have different eligibility). A 2023 Urban Institute study found that 35% of eligible immigrant families do not enroll in programs like SNAP, Medicaid, or WIC due to confusion about eligibility rules, and 24% cite fear of the "public charge" rule — which was significantly narrowed in 2022 but remains widely misunderstood.

---

## Solution

1. **Eligibility Assessment** — Step-by-step questionnaire covering immigration status, date of entry, state of residence, household income, family composition, and employment to generate a personalized eligibility report.
2. **Benefits Catalog** — Comprehensive database of federal benefits (SNAP, Medicaid, SSI, WIC, housing assistance, EITC), state benefits, and local programs with immigration-status-specific eligibility rules.
3. **Public Charge Explainer** — Clear, accurate, and current explanation of the public charge rule, which benefits are included, and which are explicitly excluded — addressing the #1 fear preventing benefit enrollment.
4. **Application Guides** — Step-by-step instructions for applying to each eligible benefit program, including required documents, office locations, and tips for the application process.
5. **Status Timeline** — Visual timeline showing how eligibility changes over time (e.g., after 5-year residency requirement is met, after naturalization) so users can plan ahead.
6. **Multi-Language Support** — Interface and guides available in Spanish, Chinese, Vietnamese, Korean, Arabic, and Haitian Creole — the most common languages among US immigrant populations.
7. **Legal Resource Directory** — Connects users with free or low-cost immigration legal services in their area for questions beyond benefit eligibility.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Benefits.gov** — Federal benefits finder but does not account for immigration status, is English-only, and does not address public charge concerns.
2. **Immigration Legal Aid Organizations** — Provide personalized guidance but are severely under-resourced (average wait time of 3-6 months) and geographically limited.
3. **Immigrant-Serving NGOs (CLINIC, NILC)** — Offer resources and policy information but not personalized eligibility tools or application guides.
4. **State DHS Websites** — Provide program information but are confusing, English-centric, and do not clarify immigration-specific eligibility rules.
5. **Immigration Attorneys** — Can advise on public charge implications but charge $200-$400/hour, making them inaccessible for most immigrants seeking benefit guidance.
