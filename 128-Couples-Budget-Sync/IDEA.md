## Problem

Money is the #1 cause of stress in relationships and the #2 cause of divorce (Institute for Divorce Financial Analysts). A 2024 Bankrate survey found that 65% of couples argue about money, with the most common fights centered on spending habits (48%), financial priorities (38%), and lack of transparency (29%). The fundamental challenge is that modern couples increasingly maintain separate finances — 42% of married couples and 72% of unmarried partners keep separate accounts — yet share expenses like rent, utilities, groceries, and savings goals. Existing budgeting apps (Mint, YNAB, Monarch Money) are designed for individuals or assume fully merged finances, forcing couples into one extreme or the other. The result: 35% of couples admit to financial infidelity (hiding purchases or debts from their partner), and 55% of couples do not have a system for splitting shared expenses fairly, leading to resentment and inequity, particularly in relationships with significant income disparity.

---

## Solution

1. **Three-Bucket System** — Separate views for "Mine," "Yours," and "Ours" — shared expenses are transparent while personal spending remains private at each partner's discretion.
2. **Fair Split Calculator** — Calculates equitable (not just 50/50) expense splitting based on income ratio, with adjustable formulas for couples who prefer proportional sharing.
3. **Shared Goals Tracker** — Joint savings goals (vacation, house down payment, emergency fund, wedding) with progress tracking and contribution visibility.
4. **Bill Split Automation** — Connects to shared bank accounts or credit cards to automatically categorize shared expenses and calculate each partner's contribution.
5. **Personal Spending Autonomy** — Each partner gets a "no questions asked" personal budget that is tracked individually and not visible to the other unless explicitly shared.
6. **Money Date Prompts** — Monthly conversation prompts for "money dates" — structured discussions about financial goals, concerns, and priorities that keep couples aligned.
7. **Income Disparity Mode** — Specialized features for couples with significantly different incomes, including proportional splitting, lifestyle leveling discussions, and equitable contribution models.
8. **Shared Transaction Feed** — Real-time feed of shared expenses so both partners see what has been spent on joint categories without needing to ask.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Monarch Money** — Excellent budgeting app with household sharing, but designed primarily for merged finances; no income-proportional splitting or personal autonomy features.
2. **YNAB (You Need A Budget)** — Powerful envelope budgeting but single-user oriented; sharing requires both partners to use the same account with full visibility.
3. **Splitwise** — Great for splitting individual expenses but designed for roommates and groups, not the ongoing financial partnership of a couple.
4. **Honeydue** — The closest competitor (couples finance app) but has struggled with reliability, limited features, and inconsistent development.
5. **Zeta** — Joint banking for couples but requires opening new accounts; does not work with existing bank relationships.

## Why This Wins

- **Respects the modern relationship** — the three-bucket system (mine/yours/ours) matches how today's couples actually manage money, unlike apps that assume full merger.
- **Income disparity is addressed** — proportional splitting features tackle the single most awkward money conversation couples face.
- **Couples subscribe together** — $5.99/mo shared between two people is $3/person, making it cheaper per user than individual budgeting apps.
- **Money date prompts drive engagement** — structured monthly conversations keep couples using the app and reduce financial conflict, improving retention.
- **Wedding industry distribution** — partnerships with wedding planning platforms, engagement gift registries, and marriage counselors create high-intent acquisition channels.
