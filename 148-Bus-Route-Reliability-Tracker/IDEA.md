## Problem

Public transit serves 34 million daily riders in the US, yet reliability is the #1 complaint across every transit system. The American Public Transportation Association reports that only 72% of bus trips arrive within 5 minutes of their scheduled time, and in some cities that figure drops to 55%. Riders waste an average of 38 minutes per week waiting for late buses — that's 33 hours per year of unproductive time. Transit agencies publish schedules but rarely share real-time reliability statistics with the public, making it impossible for riders to know which routes and time slots are consistently late. Overcrowding is another hidden problem: 28% of peak-hour riders report being unable to board their first bus because it was full, yet there's no way to predict this. The information asymmetry disproportionately affects low-income riders who are transit-dependent — 74% of regular bus riders earn under $50,000/year.

---

## Solution

1. **Route Reliability Scores** — Letter-grade reliability ratings (A through F) for every bus route based on crowdsourced actual vs. scheduled arrival data.
2. **Time-of-Day Breakdown** — Shows which departure times are most reliable for each route, helping commuters choose the 7:15 AM bus over the consistently-late 7:30 AM.
3. **Crowdsourced Reports** — One-tap reporting of late arrivals, early departures, pass-ups (bus too full), and no-shows with automatic GPS timestamp verification.
4. **Overcrowding Predictor** — Historical data on which trips tend to be full, alerting riders to leave earlier or consider alternate routes when overcrowding is likely.
5. **Real-Time Arrival Comparison** — Side-by-side display of scheduled arrival vs. current estimated arrival using GTFS-realtime data where available, supplemented by crowdsourced reports.
6. **Alternative Route Suggestions** — When your usual route is running late, suggests parallel routes, nearby stops, or multi-modal alternatives (bike-share, scooter) to reach your destination.
7. **Transit Report Card** — Monthly and annual reliability reports for transit agencies, creating public accountability through data transparency.
8. **Community Feed** — Route-specific discussion boards for riders to share real-time conditions, detour information, and tips.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Google Maps / Apple Maps** — Show transit schedules and some real-time data, but no reliability history, no crowdsourced reports, and no overcrowding information.
2. **Transit App** — Excellent real-time departure boards using official GTFS data, but no historical reliability scoring or crowdsourced quality metrics.
3. **Citymapper** — Premium multimodal planner with good transit integration, but focused on routing rather than reliability tracking.
4. **Agency-specific apps** — Individual transit agency apps (MTA, CTA, WMATA) with varying quality, no cross-system comparisons, and often poor real-time data.
5. **Moovit** — Large transit app with crowdsourced data, but reliability tracking is minimal and not the core product focus.

## Why This Wins

- **Reliability history is unique** — No existing app tells you "this route is on time 85% of the time at 8 AM but only 60% at 5 PM" — this fundamentally changes commuting decisions.
- **Crowdsourced accountability** — Public reliability scores create pressure on transit agencies to improve, benefiting the entire rider community.
- **Equity-focused design** — Free tier with full reliability data serves transit-dependent low-income riders who need this information most.
- **Overcrowding prediction** — Knowing when a bus will be too full to board is a pain point no transit app addresses, yet it's a daily frustration for thousands.
- **Recession-proof user base** — Public transit ridership actually increases during recessions as people cut car expenses, growing the addressable market during downturns.
