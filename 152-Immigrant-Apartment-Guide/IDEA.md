## Problem

Over 1 million immigrants arrive in the US annually, and one of their first and most stressful tasks is finding housing. The US rental market has uniquely American concepts — credit scores, SSNs, co-signers, application fees, background checks, 12-month leases, security deposits, renter's insurance — that don't exist in most other countries. A survey of recent immigrants found that 82% were confused by the rental application process, 64% were rejected from their first apartment due to lack of credit history, and 47% were charged excessive fees by landlords who exploited their unfamiliarity with the system. International students are particularly vulnerable: 73% arrive without a Social Security Number, which most landlords require for credit checks. Predatory practices are common — immigrant renters pay an average of 11% more than comparable non-immigrant renters for equivalent housing, largely due to information asymmetry.

---

## Solution

1. **US Rental System 101** — Plain-language explainer of every US rental concept (credit checks, SSN, co-signers, guarantors, application fees, security deposits, lease terms) with comparisons to rental systems in 20+ origin countries.
2. **No-Credit Playbook** — Step-by-step strategies for renting without US credit history: how to use international credit, offer larger deposits, find guarantor services, and identify no-credit-check listings.
3. **Application Walkthrough** — Interactive guide that walks through a typical rental application form field by field, explaining what's required and what's optional.
4. **Scam & Exploitation Detector** — Red flags to watch for: excessive application fees, discriminatory practices, illegal clauses, and common scams targeting immigrants.
5. **Document Checklist** — Country-specific list of documents to prepare before apartment hunting, including which international documents are accepted and which need translation.
6. **Rights & Protections** — Fair Housing Act protections for immigrants, what landlords legally cannot ask about, and reporting channels for discrimination.
7. **Multilingual Glossary** — Key rental vocabulary in 15+ languages with audio pronunciation, ensuring non-native English speakers understand lease terms.
8. **City-Specific Guides** — Rental market overviews for the top 25 immigrant destination cities (NYC, LA, Houston, Chicago, etc.) with average rents, neighborhoods, and commute patterns.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Zillow/Apartments.com** — General apartment search platforms with no immigrant-specific guidance, no credit history workarounds, and English-only.
2. **Immigrant advocacy websites** — Scattered free resources with rental tips, but not comprehensive, hard to find, and rarely cover the application process step-by-step.
3. **Relocation companies** — Full-service housing assistance for corporate relocations ($2,000-10,000) but priced for employer-sponsored moves, not individual immigrants.
4. **Facebook immigrant community groups** — Peer advice in specific language communities, but inconsistent quality and often outdated or city-specific only.
5. **University international student offices** — Provide some housing guidance but only for enrolled students and typically limited to campus-adjacent options.

## Why This Wins

- **Fills a genuine information void** — No single resource comprehensively explains US rental processes for people coming from different housing systems.
- **Country-of-origin contextualization** — Explaining US concepts in terms of how housing works in the user's home country creates immediate understanding.
- **Anti-exploitation focus** — Protecting vulnerable immigrants from scams and discriminatory practices provides genuine social value and strong word-of-mouth.
- **One-time purchase accessibility** — $9.99 is affordable for new arrivals who are already managing significant relocation costs.
- **Community distribution** — Immigrant communities share resources intensely; one satisfied user can drive 10+ downloads through WhatsApp groups and community organizations.
