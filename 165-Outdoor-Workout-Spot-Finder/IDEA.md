## Problem

The $96 billion fitness industry is dominated by gym memberships ($35-60/month average), but 47% of exercisers prefer working out outdoors at least part of the time, and 23% exclusively use outdoor spaces for fitness. The outdoor fitness movement has surged — calisthenics (bodyweight exercise) participation grew 42% from 2019 to 2025, and running remains the #1 exercise activity with 60 million participants. Yet finding outdoor workout infrastructure is nearly impossible: pull-up bars, dip stations, outdoor gyms, calisthenics parks, running paths, fitness trails, and exercise stations exist in thousands of parks across the US, but there's no centralized database of what exists where. Google Maps labels parks but doesn't specify if they have fitness equipment. Runners spend an average of 45 minutes researching new routes when traveling, and calisthenics practitioners report driving past suitable equipment they didn't know existed. The problem is especially acute for the 35 million Americans who've canceled gym memberships since 2020 and exercise outdoors exclusively.

---

## Solution

1. **Equipment Map** — Comprehensive map of outdoor fitness locations: pull-up bars, dip stations, calisthenics parks, outdoor gyms, fitness trails, running paths, and exercise stations.
2. **Equipment Details** — Each location includes specific equipment inventory (types of bars, heights, surfaces), condition ratings, and photos submitted by users.
3. **Workout Suggestions** — Location-specific workout routines based on available equipment: "This park has pull-up bars and parallel bars — here's a full upper body workout."
4. **Running Route Database** — Crowdsourced running routes with distance, elevation profile, surface type (paved, trail, track), and safety ratings.
5. **Traveler Mode** — Search for outdoor workout spots near your hotel or destination, perfect for maintaining routines while traveling without finding a guest pass gym.
6. **Community Ratings** — User reviews covering equipment condition, cleanliness, safety, crowding patterns by time of day, and parking availability.
7. **New Spot Submissions** — Users can add undocumented outdoor fitness locations with photos, equipment lists, and GPS coordinates, growing the database organically.
8. **Workout Groups** — Find or create outdoor workout groups at popular spots, connecting with other calisthenics enthusiasts and runners in your area.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Calisthenics Parks (website)** — Database of calisthenics parks but limited to purpose-built parks, missing park fitness stations, and a dated web-only experience with no mobile app.
2. **AllTrails** — Excellent trail finder for hiking but doesn't focus on fitness equipment, running routes in urban areas, or workout spots.
3. **Strava** — Running and cycling route tracking with segment data, but no outdoor equipment mapping and focused on logging workouts, not finding spots.
4. **Google Maps** — Parks are labeled but with zero information about fitness equipment, running paths, or outdoor gym availability.
5. **Nike Run Club / MapMyRun** — Running route suggestions but no outdoor gym equipment mapping, no calisthenics park database.

## Why This Wins

- **Comprehensive outdoor fitness mapping** — No single app maps pull-up bars, calisthenics parks, outdoor gyms, fitness trails, AND running routes together.
- **Equipment-level detail** — Knowing that a park has "2 pull-up bars (8ft), parallel dip bars, and a sit-up bench" is actionable in a way that "park with fitness area" is not.
- **Traveler use case** — Finding workout spots while traveling is an underserved pain point that creates a natural expansion path to every city.
- **Community-driven growth** — User submissions grow the database organically, and each new submission makes the app more valuable for all users in that area.
- **Free outdoor fitness advocacy** — Positioning around free, accessible fitness resonates with the growing population rejecting expensive gym memberships.
