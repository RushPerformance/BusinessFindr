## Problem

Over 41 million speeding tickets are issued annually in the United States, generating roughly $6.2 billion in fines. The average speeding ticket costs $150, but with insurance premium increases (averaging 20-25% for 3 years), the real cost exceeds $1,000 per ticket. Speed cameras are expanding rapidly — over 200 U.S. cities now use automated enforcement, up from fewer than 75 a decade ago. Drivers in unfamiliar areas are especially vulnerable, with out-of-town drivers receiving speed camera tickets at 3x the rate of locals. Existing solutions like Waze bundle speed traps into broader navigation, making the data inconsistent and often outdated.

---

## Solution

1. **Live Speed Trap Map** — Real-time map showing verified speed traps, speed cameras, red-light cameras, and police checkpoint locations.
2. **Crowdsourced Reporting** — One-tap reporting of speed traps with automatic location tagging and expiration after configurable time.
3. **Proximity Alerts** — Audio and visual alerts when approaching a known speed enforcement zone, with adjustable alert radius.
4. **Fixed Camera Database** — Comprehensive database of permanent speed and red-light cameras, updated monthly.
5. **Speed Limit Overlay** — Shows posted speed limit on-screen with speedometer comparison using GPS speed.
6. **Route Preview** — Scan your planned route for enforcement zones before you drive.
7. **Community Verification** — Users confirm or dismiss reported traps, improving data accuracy over time.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Waze** — Includes speed trap reports but bundled into full navigation app; reports expire quickly and coverage is inconsistent in suburbs.
2. **Radarbot** — Speed camera app popular in Europe, limited U.S. database, dated interface.
3. **Escort/Valentine Radar Detectors** — Hardware radar detectors cost $300-$600, illegal in some states, can't detect cameras.
4. **Trapster (discontinued)** — Was the leading speed trap app before shutting down, proving market demand exists.
5. **Google Maps** — Added basic speed trap reporting but buried in interface and community participation is low.

## Why This Wins

- **Focused purpose** — unlike Waze, this app does one thing and does it well without requiring full navigation commitment.
- **Neighborhood-level data** — targets local residential speed traps that navigation apps consistently miss.
- **Strong network effects** — each new user reporting traps makes the app more valuable for everyone.
- **$1.99/mo saves $1,000+** — one avoided ticket pays for 40+ years of premium, making the value proposition obvious.
- **Legal in most jurisdictions** — speed trap alerts are legal everywhere radar detectors are, covering 49 states.
