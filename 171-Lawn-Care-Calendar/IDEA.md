## Problem

The U.S. landscaping industry is valued at $130 billion, with homeowners spending an average of $500-$1,200/year on lawn care. There are over 80 million home lawns in the United States. New homeowners — roughly 5.5 million per year — are especially lost, often applying the wrong fertilizer at the wrong time or mowing at incorrect heights. Warm-season and cool-season grasses have completely different care schedules, yet generic lawn care advice doesn't differentiate. Mistimed fertilizer applications waste $200+/year and can burn grass. Overwatering costs the average homeowner $150-$300/year in excess water bills while promoting disease.

---

## Solution

1. **Grass Type Identifier** — Select or photo-identify your grass type (Bermuda, Fescue, Bluegrass, Zoysia, St. Augustine, etc.).
2. **Climate Zone Calibration** — Uses USDA hardiness zone and local weather data to set region-appropriate schedules.
3. **Monthly Task Calendar** — Specific, date-appropriate tasks: when to fertilize, overseed, aerate, dethatch, and apply pre-emergent.
4. **Mowing Height Guide** — Optimal mowing heights by grass type and season, with reminders to adjust mower settings.
5. **Watering Schedule** — Irrigation recommendations based on rainfall data, soil type, and grass needs.
6. **Weed & Disease Alerts** — Seasonal warnings for common issues (crabgrass, grubs, brown patch) with treatment timing.
7. **Product Recommendations** — Specific fertilizer N-P-K ratios and product suggestions for each application window.
8. **Progress Photos** — Monthly photo journal to track lawn improvement over seasons.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **My Lawn by Scotts** — Free app from Scotts Miracle-Gro; decent but heavily biased toward selling Scotts products, limited grass type coverage.
2. **LawnStarter** — Marketplace for hiring lawn care pros, not a DIY tool.
3. **Planta** — Plant care app that includes some lawn features, but primarily focused on houseplants and gardens.
4. **r/lawncare (Reddit)** — Active community with great advice but unstructured, requires self-directed research.
5. **Local Extension Office Resources** — Authoritative but PDFs and websites, not actionable mobile-first tools.

## Why This Wins
