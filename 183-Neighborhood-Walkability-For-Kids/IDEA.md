## Problem

Pedestrian fatalities involving children under 14 total roughly 250 per year in the United States, with another 18,000 child pedestrian injuries. Only 13% of children walk to school today compared to 48% in 1969, largely due to parents' safety concerns about traffic, sidewalk gaps, and unsafe crossings. Walk Score — the dominant walkability metric — rates neighborhoods by proximity to amenities but completely ignores child-specific safety factors like crossing guards, playground density, traffic calming measures, and sex offender registries. Parents relocating to new cities have no data-driven way to evaluate which neighborhoods are truly safe for kids to walk, bike, and play.

---

## Solution

1. **Kid-Safe Walk Score** — Proprietary 0-100 score incorporating sidewalk coverage, traffic speeds, crossing guards, playground proximity, and street lighting.
2. **School Route Analysis** — Evaluates the walking route from any address to assigned schools for safety factors.
3. **Playground Map** — Locations, equipment types, age-appropriateness ratings, and crowdsourced condition reports for nearby playgrounds.
4. **Traffic Calming Inventory** — Maps speed bumps, crosswalks, school zones, and traffic signals along common walking routes.
5. **Community Reports** — Parents report unsafe conditions (missing sidewalks, dangerous intersections, speeding areas) with photo evidence.
6. **Neighborhood Comparison** — Side-by-side comparison of up to 5 neighborhoods on child-friendliness metrics.
7. **Safety Alerts** — Notifications about road construction, crossing guard schedule changes, or new hazards on school routes.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Walk Score** — Industry-standard walkability metric, but measures adult amenity proximity, not child safety.
2. **GreatSchools** — Rates schools but not the walking routes or neighborhood safety features around them.
3. **Nextdoor** — Community discussions about safety but anecdotal, inconsistent, and hard to compare across neighborhoods.
4. **Niche.com** — Neighborhood grades including "family-friendliness" but based on demographics, not physical safety infrastructure.
