> Crowdsourced map of participating houses for trick-or-treating with candy ratings, scare levels, and decorated house tours.

---

## Problem

Halloween spending in the United States exceeds $12 billion annually, with 68% of Americans participating in trick-or-treating or handing out candy. Yet the experience has declined — only 30% of houses in the average neighborhood participate in trick-or-treating, down from 55% two decades ago. Families waste significant trick-or-treating time walking to dark, non-participating houses. Parents of young children want to know which neighborhoods are safest and most active. Meanwhile, homeowners who invest $50-$200 in candy and decorations want trick-or-treaters to actually visit. There's a coordination failure: participating houses can't signal their availability, and families can't find them efficiently.

---

## Solution

1. **Participation Map** — Homeowners mark their house as "participating" with hours, creating a real-time map of active houses.
2. **Candy Ratings** — Community ratings for candy quality (full-size bars get 5 stars, loose candy corn gets 1).
3. **Scare Level Tags** — Labels houses as "kid-friendly," "mildly spooky," or "haunted house level" so parents choose appropriately.
4. **Route Planner** — Generates an efficient trick-or-treat walking route through the densest clusters of participating houses.
5. **Decoration Showcase** — Photo gallery of decorated houses, driving visits to the best displays.
6. **Neighborhood Rankings** — Rates neighborhoods by participation density, candy quality, and decoration effort.
7. **Safety Features** — Well-lit route suggestions, group coordination for parent walking groups, emergency contact integration.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Nextdoor** — Some neighborhoods post trick-or-treat info but inconsistent, no map view, no ratings.
2. **Trunk-or-Treat Events** — Organized parking lot events that solve safety concerns but eliminate the house-to-house experience.
3. **Local Facebook Groups** — Community-posted info about decorations and participating streets, fragmented and unstructured.
4. **TrickOrTreatMap.org** — Simple participation map concept; minimal features and low adoption.

---

## Why This Wins

- **Solves a real coordination problem** — connecting candy-givers with trick-or-treaters benefits both sides.
- **Inherently viral** — parents share with other parents; one user per neighborhood catalyzes block-wide adoption.
- **Annual engagement** — users return every October creating predictable seasonal engagement.
- **Nostalgia + community** — taps into desire to revive the "golden age" of trick-or-treating.
- **Minimal infrastructure** — simple map + user-generated data, very low server and development costs.
