## Problem

Over 90% of American households own a microwave, yet wattage varies from 600W to 1,200W — a 2x range that dramatically affects cooking times. Food packaging assumes a 1,000W-1,100W microwave, but millions of households (especially dorm rooms, offices, and older homes) have 700W-900W microwaves. Cooking at the package-stated time in a lower-wattage microwave leaves food cold in the center. Overcompensating leads to rubbery, dried-out food. The average person microwaves food 4-5 times per week, and an estimated 40% of microwave meals are over- or under-heated due to wattage mismatch. Most people don't know their microwave's wattage and wouldn't know how to adjust times if they did.

---

## Solution

1. **Wattage Calculator** — Helps users determine their microwave wattage using a simple water-heating test if unknown.
2. **Time Converter** — Input the package time and wattage; get the adjusted cooking time for your specific microwave.
3. **Food-Specific Guides** — Optimal times and power levels for common microwave tasks: defrosting, reheating pizza, steaming vegetables, cooking potatoes.
4. **Power Level Guide** — When to use 50%, 70%, or 100% power for different foods (most people only use full power).
5. **Standing Time Reminders** — Timer includes recommended standing time for continued cooking after the microwave stops.
6. **Favorites** — Save frequently microwaved items with your calculated time for one-tap access.
7. **Tips & Tricks** — Techniques most people don't know: doughnut arrangement, damp paper towel for bread, etc.

## Existing Alternatives

1. **Food Package Instructions** — Standard timing for 1,000W-1,100W microwaves, no personalization.
2. **Google Search** — "How long to microwave [food]" returns generic results without wattage adjustment.
3. **Recipe Apps** — Occasionally include microwave instructions but rarely address wattage differences.
4. **Trial and Error** — The current approach for most people, resulting in cold centers and rubbery edges.
