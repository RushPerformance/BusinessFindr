# Micro-Savings Rounder

> Rounds up every purchase to the nearest dollar and sweeps the difference into a savings account — with goals, streaks, and visual progress for people who "can't save."

## The Problem
56% of Americans can't cover an unexpected $1,000 expense (Bankrate, 2023). The median savings account balance for under-35s is $1,200. The problem isn't that people don't want to save — it's that traditional saving requires willpower and feels like deprivation. Round-up apps proved the concept (Acorns has 10M+ users), but Acorns pushes investing ($3-5/mo fee + investment complexity) when most people need emergency savings first, not a brokerage account.

## The Solution
1. Connect bank account/debit card
2. Round up every purchase to the nearest dollar (or $2, $5 — user choice)
3. Sweep round-ups into a savings account (FDIC-insured via banking partner)
4. Goal setting: "Emergency fund: $1,000" with visual progress bar
5. Savings streaks and milestones (gamification without shame)
6. "Boost" feature: add extra savings on payday or when you resist impulse purchases
7. Financial education nudges: "You've saved $127 this month — that's 2 months of Netflix"
8. Savings withdrawal with gentle friction ("Are you sure? You're 74% to your goal")

## Existing Alternatives
- **Acorns**: $3-5/mo, invests round-ups (not savings), adds complexity
- **Qapital**: $3-5/mo, gamified saving but pivoted to banking suite
- **Digit**: $5/mo, AI-based saving but removes user control
- **Chime/Cash App**: Have round-up features but as a side feature of banking
- **Traditional savings accounts**: Require manual transfers

## Why This Wins
- Savings-first (not investing-first) addresses the actual need for most users
- Lowest price point in category ($3/mo vs. $3-5/mo for less focused competitors)
- Simplicity: does one thing, does it well — no investing complexity
- Goal visualization and gamification drive engagement without investing risk
- Under-banked and young adult demographics are underserved
