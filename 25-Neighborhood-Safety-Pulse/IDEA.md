## The Problem
Moving to a new neighborhood is one of the highest-stakes decisions people make, yet safety data is hard to find and harder to interpret. Crime maps (CrimeMapping, SpotCrime) show raw incident dots but don't provide context: is this neighborhood getting safer or more dangerous? How does it compare to the city average? Nextdoor and Citizen amplify fear with real-time alerts that make every neighborhood feel dangerous. Parents, women living alone, and new-to-city transplants need objective, contextualized safety data — not noise.

## The Solution
1. Safety score (1-100) for any US address, calculated from public data
2. Trend analysis: is this area getting safer or more dangerous over 1/3/5 years?
3. Comparison tool: compare two neighborhoods side by side
4. Category breakdown: property crime vs. violent crime vs. traffic incidents
5. Time-of-day heat maps: when is this area safest/least safe?
6. Community reports (structured, not free-form rants like Nextdoor)
7. Customizable alerts: notify me of incidents within X blocks of my home/work
8. Walk score integration: safety + walkability combined for a holistic picture
9. School safety data for parents

## Existing Alternatives
- **Citizen**: Real-time incident alerts, fear-mongering UX, no historical trends
- **Nextdoor**: Community posts about crime, no data-driven analysis
- **CrimeMapping/SpotCrime**: Raw crime data maps, no scoring or context
- **AreaVibes**: Livability scores but safety data is shallow
- **Zillow/Redfin**: Basic crime data, not their focus

## Why This Wins
- "Context, not fear" positioning differentiates from Citizen/Nextdoor
- Trend analysis (getting safer vs. more dangerous) is a unique, high-value feature
- Comparison tool serves the specific moment when people are choosing between neighborhoods
- Structured community reports avoid the toxicity of Nextdoor comment threads
- Strong utility for renters, homebuyers, and parents — covers a large TAM
