# Tutor Match

> Hyperlocal tutoring marketplace connecting students with verified neighborhood tutors — focused on K-12, test prep, and adult skills with 15-minute booking.

## The Problem
The US tutoring market is $12B but highly fragmented. Parents spend hours finding tutors through word-of-mouth, Craigslist, or expensive platforms like Wyzant ($60-80/hour). College students and retired teachers want to tutor but lack distribution. Existing platforms take 30-40% commission (Wyzant) or charge tutors monthly fees (Tutor.com). Hyperlocal matching (same neighborhood, in-person option) is underserved as platforms push online-only.

## The Solution
1. Students/parents search by subject, grade level, location, price range
2. Tutors create profiles with credentials, availability, and rates
3. Background check verification badge
4. In-person + online options with distance radius
5. 15-minute booking: view availability, book, and confirm instantly
6. Session tracking: what was covered, homework assigned, progress notes
7. Parent dashboard: see all sessions, notes, and progress across subjects
8. Rating and review system
9. Group tutoring option for lower per-student cost

## Market Size
- **TAM**: $12.1B — US private tutoring market (Technavio)
- **SAM**: $2.4B — marketplace/platform-based tutoring
- **SOM**: $1.2M — $8M GMV × 15% take rate

## Existing Alternatives
- **Wyzant**: Largest marketplace, 30-40% commission, expensive for students
- **Varsity Tutors**: $50-80/hour, premium pricing
- **Tutor.com**: Monthly subscription model, impersonal
- **Care.com**: Has tutoring but it's a side feature
- **Craigslist/Facebook**: No verification, no scheduling, no quality control

## Why This Wins
- 15% commission vs. 30-40% at Wyzant means better prices for students and pay for tutors
- Hyperlocal focus enables in-person tutoring (which parents prefer for younger kids)
- 15-minute booking removes friction from the discovery-to-session process
- Progress tracking gives parents visibility they don't get with informal tutoring
- Two-sided network effects create a defensible moat
