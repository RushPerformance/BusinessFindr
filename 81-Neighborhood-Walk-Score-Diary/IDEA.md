## The Problem
Walk Score (the dominant walkability metric) rates neighborhoods based on proximity to amenities using mapping data — but it doesn't account for sidewalk conditions, safety (lighting, crosswalks), hills, construction detours, or actual pedestrian experience. A neighborhood can score 85 on Walk Score but have crumbling sidewalks and no crosswalks. The $2.8B walkability and urban mobility analytics market relies on static data, not lived experience. 80% of Americans say walkability is important when choosing where to live (National Association of Realtors), yet there's no tool that captures real, ground-level walkability data from actual walkers.

## The Solution
1. Walk logging: record walking trips with GPS route, duration, distance, and purpose (errand, exercise, commute)
2. Personal walkability scoring based on your actual trips: sidewalk quality, safety rating, accessibility, pleasantness
3. Route condition reporting: mark issues like broken sidewalks, missing crosswalks, poor lighting, or construction
4. Trend tracking: how your walking habits change by season, weather, and day of week
5. Neighborhood comparison: see how your walkability experience compares to others in the same area
6. Community reports: aggregate data for neighborhood associations, city council presentations, or real estate decisions
7. Health integration: step counts, calories, and distance synced with Apple Health / Google Fit

## Existing Alternatives
- **Walk Score**: Industry standard but uses proximity data only, no actual walking experience
- **Strava**: Tracks walking/running routes but focused on athletic performance, not walkability assessment
- **Google Maps walking directions**: Shows routes but no condition data, safety info, or personal scoring
- **AllTrails**: Trail-focused, not urban neighborhood walking
- **City planning surveys**: Capture walkability data but are infrequent, expensive, and not consumer-facing

## Why This Wins
- Ground-truth walkability data is fundamentally more useful than proximity-based scores
- Community reporting gives neighborhoods ammunition for infrastructure improvements
- Real estate value: homebuyers could use personal walk data to evaluate neighborhoods before moving
- Health tracking integration makes it a dual-purpose app (walkability + fitness)
- Data has B2B value: aggregated walkability data is valuable to city planners, real estate developers, and insurance companies
