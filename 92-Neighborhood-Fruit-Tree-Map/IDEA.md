## The Problem
An estimated 10 billion pounds of fruit goes unharvested in the US annually (USDA), much of it on public trees, community plantings, and overhanging branches from private property. The $1.8B urban foraging and local food movement is growing rapidly, especially among millennials and Gen Z interested in sustainability. But urban foragers face three problems: finding fruit trees (most go unnoticed), knowing when they're ripe (harvest windows vary by species and microclimate), and understanding what's legal to pick (public vs. private property, local ordinances). Existing foraging maps like Falling Fruit have data but are outdated, unmaintained, and lack real-time ripeness information.

## The Solution
1. Interactive map of public and shared fruit trees marked by species, location, and access notes
2. Crowdsourced contributions: users add trees with photos, species identification, and property status (public, shared, community garden)
3. Harvest calendar: species-specific ripeness windows adjusted for your local climate zone
4. Ripeness reports: users update current condition ("apples are ready," "pears still green") for real-time accuracy
5. Foraging etiquette guide: legal considerations, best practices, and how to ask private property owners for permission
6. Species identification: photo-based ID for unknown fruit trees
7. Recipe suggestions based on what's currently in season nearby
8. Community harvest events: coordinate group picking sessions to prevent waste

## Existing Alternatives
- **Falling Fruit**: The most well-known foraging map, but data is 5-10 years old and rarely updated
- **iNaturalist**: Plant identification but not focused on edible species or harvest timing
- **Local foraging Facebook groups**: Active communities but information is scattered and hard to search
- **Foraging books**: Static guides that don't map specific locations or provide real-time ripeness data
- **Google Maps**: Can find "fruit trees" but no species data, harvest info, or community contributions

## Why This Wins
- Real-time ripeness reports are the killer feature — knowing a tree exists is useless without knowing when to pick
- Legal/etiquette guidance addresses the #1 hesitation people have about urban foraging
- Community harvest events prevent waste and build social connection around free food
- Crowdsourced data model means the map gets more valuable with every user
- Strong alignment with sustainability, food security, and zero-waste movements driving organic growth
