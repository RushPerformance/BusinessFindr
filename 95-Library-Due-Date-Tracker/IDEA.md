## The Problem
There are 9,000+ public library systems in the US serving 171 million cardholders (IMLS). Library patrons check out an average of 8.4 items per year, but late returns remain a persistent problem — libraries collected $340 million in fines annually before the fine-free movement began (many libraries still charge fines). 42% of library users have paid a late fee in the past year (Pew Research). The problem intensifies for people who use multiple library systems (city, county, university, digital) — each has its own app, website, due dates, and renewal policies. Parents managing children's library cards multiply the complexity further.

## The Solution
1. Unified dashboard: see all checked-out items across multiple library systems in one view
2. Due date reminders: customizable alerts at 3 days, 1 day, and day-of for each item
3. One-tap renewal: renew eligible items directly from the app (where library APIs allow)
4. Late fee calculator: see potential fines accumulating if items aren't returned on time
5. Hold notifications: alerts when held items become available for pickup
6. Family mode: manage multiple library cards (kids, spouse) in one account
7. Reading history: track what you've borrowed over time for personal records
8. Library hours and location finder with real-time open/closed status

## Existing Alternatives
- **Individual library apps**: Most library systems have their own app, but patrons using multiple systems need multiple apps
- **Libby / OverDrive**: Digital lending platforms with due dates, but only for e-books and audiobooks, not physical items
- **Library email reminders**: Some systems send emails but they're inconsistent and often end up in spam
- **Phone calendar reminders**: Manual entry for each item — tedious and error-prone
- **Goodreads**: Tracks what you've read but has no library integration or due date features

## Why This Wins
- Multi-library unification is the killer feature — no app consolidates due dates across systems
- Family card management solves a real pain point for parents tracking 3-5 cards
- Even at $0.99/mo, preventing one late fee ($1-5 per item) makes the app pay for itself
- Fine-free movement is growing but not universal — millions still face late fees
- Library patrons are a loyal, engaged audience who check out items regularly
