## The Problem
The US birding market is worth $4.7 billion annually, with 57 million Americans identifying as birdwatchers (US Fish & Wildlife Service). An estimated 59 million households maintain bird feeders. But most backyard birders have no systematic way to track what species visit their feeders, when they appear, and what attracts them. Birding apps like Merlin and eBird focus on field identification and checklists for birding excursions — not the stationary, daily, repetitive experience of backyard feeder watching. Backyard birders want to know: Are cardinals coming more or less this year? When do my hummingbirds arrive in spring? Which seed attracts the most variety? These questions require longitudinal data that no app currently captures from a feeder-centric perspective.

## The Solution
1. Quick species logging: tap to log a bird sighting at your feeder with species, count, and time
2. Photo ID assist: snap a photo for species identification help using a bird recognition database
3. Seasonal pattern tracking: charts showing which species visit by month, week, and time of day
4. Seed recommendations: which seeds and feeders attract specific target species in your region
5. Year-over-year comparison: "You had 40% more goldfinch visits this March vs. last March"
6. Feeder inventory: track your feeders by type (tube, platform, suet, hummingbird) and which seed each contains
7. Feeder maintenance reminders: cleaning schedules to prevent disease spread (a real concern — salmonellosis kills thousands of birds annually)
8. Regional species calendar: expected arrival/departure dates for migratory species at your location

## Existing Alternatives
- **Merlin Bird ID (Cornell Lab)**: Excellent free bird ID app but designed for field birding, not feeder tracking
- **eBird**: Global bird observation database but focused on location-based checklists, not feeder activity logs
- **Audubon Bird Guide**: Species information and ID, but no personal tracking or feeder management
- **Pen-and-paper bird journals**: Beloved by birders but can't generate trend analysis or seasonal charts
- **Trail cameras / bird cams**: Capture photos but don't identify species, track patterns, or recommend seeds

## Why This Wins
- Feeder-centric design fills a gap between field birding apps and backyard birders' actual behavior
- Seed recommendations create actionable value — most birders want to attract specific species
- Year-over-year trend tracking turns casual feeding into citizen science
- Feeder cleaning reminders serve bird health and differentiate from pure tracking apps
- Passionate community with high word-of-mouth potential — birders love sharing their sightings
